## SLIDE 6 — What is climate change?

Let's start with the basics, because we want a shared starting point. Climate change refers to long-term alterations in temperature, precipitation, and other atmospheric conditions. The crucial word in there is *long-term* — we're not talking about whether it rained yesterday, or whether last summer was unusually hot. We're talking about shifts that play out across decades. And the driver, overwhelmingly, is human activity — primarily the burning of fossil fuels and the clearing of forests. Those activities release greenhouse gases that trap heat in the atmosphere. The science on that is settled. Where the conversation gets really interesting — and where this course lives — is in what all of that actually means for our physical, mental, and emotional lives.

## SLIDE 7 — Impacts

Climate change isn't a single impact — it's a whole cascade of them, all interacting with each other. We're talking about rising sea levels, more frequent and severe extreme weather, food and water insecurity, direct human health risks like heat stress and respiratory illness, biodiversity loss, forced migration, economic disruption, and damage to the infrastructure that holds daily life together. Each of these has its own emotional signature. Each of these touches real lives. As we move through the rest of the course, I want you to keep this picture in mind, because every climate emotion we name is really a response to one or more of these realities playing out somewhere — sometimes far away, sometimes right outside the window.

## SLIDE 8 — Planetary Health

Next, I want to introduce you to a framework called planetary health. It's a way of thinking that bridges a lot of disciplines — earth sciences, ecology, psychology, public health — and its core claim is simple but actually pretty profound: human wellbeing is deeply connected to the health of the environment. We can't be healthy on a sick planet. Clean air, safe water, healthy food, stable conditions — these are the building blocks of human flourishing. The figure on this slide shows nine planetary boundaries — things like biodiversity, freshwater, atmospheric chemistry — that need to stay within safe ranges for humanity to thrive. Each circle is a snapshot at a different point in time. When a wedge crosses past the inner ring, that boundary has been breached. In 2009, three were crossed. By 2025, seven of nine are. That's the urgency we're working with.

## SLIDE 9 — Indigenous Perspectives

Now I want to broaden the lens. Western science describes climate change in terms of emissions, temperatures, and tipping points. But Indigenous perspectives describe something deeper — a disruption of relationships. From many Indigenous viewpoints, climate change isn't only a scientific or ecological crisis. It's a reflection of broken relationships between humans and the land, and between humans and what are sometimes called our non-human relations — the animals, the rivers, the mountains. Where Western frameworks tend to focus on mechanisms, Indigenous teachings often speak to the spiritual, ethical, and relational consequences of all of this. So the call to address climate change becomes, in this view, a call to renew relationships — with land, with each other, and with the generations to come. This relational lens matters. It expands our understanding of climate health to include social and emotional wellbeing as inseparable from everything else.

## SLIDE 13 — Trauma-Informed Care

Before we start naming emotions, I want to set a frame: trauma-informed care. Climate distress is rarely a fresh wound. It usually layers on top of other experiences of loss, displacement, or harm — and that's true whether we're talking about ourselves or about people we support. So trauma-informed care offers six widely-used principles to keep in mind. The first is safety — both physical and emotional. The second is trustworthiness and transparency — being clear about what to expect and why. Third is peer support — drawing on shared experience to reduce isolation. Fourth is collaboration and mutuality — flattening the hierarchies that can creep into helping relationships. Fifth is empowerment, voice, and choice. And sixth is attention to cultural, historical, and gender considerations — because identity and history shape both experience and access. These principles apply to how you engage with your own emotions, and to how you support other people's. Carry them with you through everything that follows.

## SLIDE 14 — Emotions, Wellbeing, Mental Health

Now a quick detour into definitions. Our understanding of health has evolved a lot over time, and that evolution matters here. The old biomedical model focused narrowly on the absence of disease — you were healthy if you weren't sick. The World Health Organization's 1948 definition expanded that to include complete physical, mental, and social wellbeing. George Engel's biopsychosocial model in 1977 layered in psychological factors as essential. And many Indigenous health systems have, for much longer, held an integrated view — physical, mental, emotional, spiritual, and relational, all together, all inseparable from the land. The point I want you to take from this is simple: emotions are not problems to be eliminated. They are meaningful signals about the state of our relationships with the world.

---

## SLIDE 15 — Constructed Emotion

I want to pause briefly on a theoretical point that I find useful. There's no single, settled science of what emotions actually are. Older basic-emotion theories said there's a small set of universal feelings — fear, anger, sadness, joy — each with its own facial expression and its own brain signature. Newer work, most famously by the psychologist Lisa Feldman Barrett, argues something different. Her theory of constructed emotion suggests that emotions aren't pre-wired reflexes — they're actually assembled in the moment. Your brain takes bodily sensations, prior experience, language, and cultural concepts, and constructs an emotional experience that fits the situation. So two people facing the very same wildfire smoke might construct quite different emotions — one might feel grief, another rage, a third a kind of numb resolve — depending on what their body, their history, and their culture make available to them. The takeaway is modest but important: climate emotions are real, meaningful, and variable. They're not a template that looks the same in everyone.

---

## SLIDE 16 — Emotions vs. Mental Health Conditions

One more crucial distinction before we get into the spectrum. Emotions are not the same as mental health conditions. Emotions are natural, transient responses to circumstances, and they serve adaptive functions — they're telling us something. Mental health conditions, by contrast, are persistent, diagnosable patterns that significantly impair daily functioning and that typically require professional intervention. This course intentionally focuses on emotions, and the reason is important: we don't want to pathologize healthy responses to genuine threats. Feeling grief about a vanishing forest isn't a disorder. It's an accurate response to loss. Now, some climate distress does cross into clinical territory, and we'll talk about that in Module Four. But our default frame here is: emotions are signals, not symptoms.

## SLIDE 17 — Spectrum overview

So here we are — about to walk through the spectrum itself. Researchers and practitioners have mapped a broad range of emotional responses to climate change, and we're going to work with nine overlapping categories. I want to emphasize that word *overlapping*, because they really aren't mutually exclusive — most people carry several of these at once, and the mix shifts over time. We'll go through each one with the same structure: how it shows up in the body, in our thoughts, and in our behaviour; what tends to help when you're feeling it yourself; and what tends to help when you're supporting someone else through it. Don't try to memorize all nine right now. Just notice which ones you recognize, in yourself or in the people around you.

## SLIDE 18 — Worry & Anxiety

Number one on our list: worry and anxiety. This is, for most people, the most common entry point into climate emotions. Picture someone scrolling wildfire coverage at midnight, unable to put their phone down — that's worry in action. How does it show up? Circling thoughts after the news, trouble sleeping, restlessness, chest tightness, compulsive doomscrolling, irritability with people who seem unconcerned, those endless what-if loops about the future. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Naming it — worry is information, not weakness. Setting a boundary on your news intake, so you're not in it all day. And pairing the worry with one concrete action, however small, to convert helplessness into agency. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Validate first — don't reassure them with platitudes. Ask what they need before you offer it. And let them know they're not alone, because just naming the experience tends to reduce shame.

---

## SLIDE 19 — Anticipatory Stress & Trauma

Number two: anticipatory stress and trauma responses. This one goes a layer deeper than worry. Picture someone in a wildfire region, every August, bracing for the next evacuation order — the body is already in alarm mode before anything has actually happened. How does it show up? Hypervigilance during weather events, intrusive memories or images, nightmares, startle reactions, emotional numbness, avoidance of certain places, dread about events that haven't yet happened, and somatic symptoms like gut tension, headaches, or chronic fatigue. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Grounding techniques — the five-four-three-two-one senses exercise, slow breathing, cold water on the hands. Routines that reinforce a sense of safety. Gentle exposure rather than full avoidance. And professional support if symptoms persist beyond a few weeks, because trauma responds well to evidence-based therapy. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Create predictability and choice. Don't pressure them to talk. Be present without trying to fix anything. Watch for anniversary effects of past disasters. And encourage professional support gently when it seems appropriate.

---

## SLIDE 20 — Grief & Solastalgia

Number three: grief and solastalgia. The word *solastalgia* was coined by the philosopher Glenn Albrecht, and it describes a homesickness-like distress that you feel when your home environment is changing around you while you're still living in it. Picture someone walking past the dry creek where they swam as a child, and feeling that throat-catch of loss — that's solastalgia. How does it show up? Sudden waves of sadness triggered by familiar landscapes, mourning for species or seasons that have changed, ambiguous grief that has no clear closure, withdrawal from places you once loved, a kind of phantom-limb sense of what's been lost. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Naming the loss out loud — climate grief is rarely socially sanctioned, so the act of naming really matters. Ritualizing where possible — write, walk, photograph, plant. Giving yourself permission to grieve without rushing for a fix. And finding a climate grief circle if you can. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Resist the urge to silver-line. Sit with the loss the way you would for any bereavement. Acknowledge the legitimacy of grieving non-human beings or places. And mark losses ceremonially when it feels right.

---

## SLIDE 21 — Guilt & Shame

Number four: guilt and shame. Picture someone agonizing over a flight they took last year, or feeling like a "bad person" because they drive a car. How does it show up? Rumination over past consumption choices, hyper-focus on personal carbon footprint, self-criticism that tips into paralysis, perfectionism about lifestyle changes, or defensive responses when others raise climate topics. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Distinguishing guilt — which is about an action — from shame, which is about the self. Guilt can motivate; shame more often paralyses. Locate your contribution in proportion to the structural drivers, because frankly, your individual carbon footprint is not the issue. Redirect rumination into one chosen action that aligns with your values. And practise self-compassion explicitly — you may need to say it to yourself out loud. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Don't amplify their personal guilt as a motivator — it usually backfires. Name the structural responsibility alongside individual choice. Model imperfection in yourself. And redirect the conversation toward collective action rather than individual purity.

---

## SLIDE 22 — Anger & Rage

Number five: anger and rage. Picture someone snapping at a colleague for printing single-sided, and then realizing later that the fury isn't really about the paper. How does it show up? Heat or tightness in the chest when reading about inaction, snapping at family or colleagues, cynicism toward institutions, fantasies of confrontation, difficulty being present in everyday life because the wider injustice keeps intruding, burnout from sustained outrage, and alienation from people who seem complacent. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Respect the anger as a moral signal — it's telling you something important. Channel it into structured action — organizing, advocacy, writing — rather than diffuse fuming. Build in recovery so the anger doesn't tip you into burnout. And separate moral clarity — the system *is* unjust — from interpersonal hostility — the cashier in front of you is *not* the system. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Validate the anger without trying to defuse it. Help them locate where the anger most accurately belongs. Connect them with organizing communities that can hold the energy. And watch for signs of burnout.

---

## SLIDE 23 — Isolation & Alienation

Number six: isolation, loneliness, and alienation. Picture someone holding back their climate worry at the family dinner table, because no one else will engage with it. How does it show up? Feeling unable to bring up climate topics with family, friends, or colleagues. Climate silence in social settings. A quiet sense that you're grieving alone. Drifting away from people who seem disengaged. Online overuse to find others who get it — sometimes that helps, sometimes it's corrosive. And shame for caring "too much." What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Seek out spaces where the topic is welcomed — climate cafés, faith groups, peer circles. Find one trusted person you can be honest with. Recognize that the silence reflects a social condition, not a personal failure on your part. Don't take it as evidence that you're alone. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Just acknowledging climate concerns out loud breaks the silence. Ask how they're doing about it specifically, rather than waiting for them to bring it up. Share resources or local groups. And model the conversation, so others learn it's possible to have.

---

## SLIDE 24 — Denial & Disengagement

Number seven: denial and disengagement. This one often gets misread as a moral failing. It's better understood as a protective psychological mechanism. Picture someone who changes the subject every time the wildfire smoke comes up, or laughs it off. How does it show up? Changing the subject when climate is raised, dismissive humour, intellectualizing without feeling, hyper-focus on solutions to avoid sitting with the problem, "I just can't think about that right now," disengagement after periods of intense engagement, and sometimes outright rejection of the science. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Notice when avoidance is briefly protective versus when it's calcifying into something more permanent. Re-engage gently — through a person you trust, a story rather than a statistic, or one small action. And recognize that disengagement after prolonged intense engagement is often burnout, not apathy. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Avoid confrontation and information dumps — those tend to deepen denial, not loosen it. Ask open questions about what they care about, and connect from there. Share your own emotional truth rather than facts. And give it time — movement here is slow and non-linear.

---

## SLIDE 25 — Ecoparalysis & Fatalism

Number eight: ecoparalysis and fatalism. This is what happens when caring runs out of road. Picture someone who cares deeply but feels so flattened by the scale of the crisis that they've stopped trying — and notice that this is different from denial. The person sees the problem clearly. They just can't move. How does it show up? "What's the point" thinking, abandoning practices they used to hold to, doom-leaning humour, oscillation between bursts of action and total withdrawal, decision fatigue around any climate-relevant choice, drift toward nihilistic communities online. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Shrink the unit of action — pick one tiny achievable thing and do it. Prioritize collective action over solo effort, because collective efficacy is one of the known antidotes to fatalism. Limit prolonged exposure to doomist voices. And restore the basics — rest and nutrition, because ecoparalysis often rides on top of physical depletion. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Don't try to argue them into hope. Do something small alongside them. Share concrete examples of meaningful change — local, partial, honest. And watch for signs that paralysis is tipping into clinical depression.

---

## SLIDE 26 — Hope & Resilience

Number nine: hope and resilience. We end the spectrum here on purpose. Picture someone leaving a community planting day tired but lighter, knowing the work was small but real. How does it show up? Renewed energy after time in community, the capacity to hold difficulty alongside possibility, motivation that survives setbacks, a willingness to engage in projects whose outcomes are uncertain, gratitude that can coexist with grief, and a felt sense that what one does matters even when it isn't enough on its own. What helps when you're feeling it yourself? Treat hope as a practice, not a mood — return to it deliberately. Anchor it in concrete, witnessed change rather than vague optimism. Surround yourself with people who model engaged hope. And build in rest and joy, because sustainable hope requires energy reserves. What helps when you're supporting someone else? Don't impose hope on someone in grief or anger — let it emerge in its own time. Share specific examples that honour both the reality and the possibility. Celebrate small wins together. And model the truth that hope and despair can coexist without one cancelling the other out.

## SLIDE 30 — Biopsychosocial Model

Let's start with the biopsychosocial model. The basic claim is that climate emotions aren't random reactions — they're functional responses to real threats, and they're shaped by three interacting layers. There's a biological layer — your genetic predisposition, your physiological stress responses. There's a psychological layer — your personality, your coping style, the cognitive appraisals you make about what's happening. And there's a social layer — your community supports, your cultural context, your socioeconomic status. Now, the important thing about these three layers is that they don't operate separately. They interact, constantly, and the emotional response you actually experience is the product of that interaction. No single layer explains the whole picture.

---

## SLIDE 31 — Social & Environmental Drivers

Let's drill into the social and environmental layer for a moment, because that's where a lot of the variation lives. The single strongest driver of climate distress is direct exposure to a climate-related event — a wildfire, a flood, a hurricane, an extreme heat episode. But indirect exposure matters too — through media consumption, anticipatory worry about future events, and ongoing awareness of environmental degradation. There are six factors I want you to keep on your radar. The first is geographic vulnerability — flood zones, fire-prone areas, urban heat islands. The second is socioeconomic status, which shapes your access to adaptation resources. The third is the quality and availability of your social support networks. The fourth is your media exposure and information environment. The fifth is your connection to land, culture, and place. And the sixth — often underestimated — is your access to green spaces and to nature itself.

---

## SLIDE 32 — Lifecourse Perspective

Climate emotions also shift across the arc of a life. Each developmental stage brings its own concerns, its own resources, its own constraints. Picture a fifteen-year-old who's just had panic attacks after a unit on rising sea levels, but who has also organized a school clothing-swap with friends. Picture a twenty-eight-year-old who's just switched careers into climate work, and who is now newly unsure about whether to have kids. Picture a forty-seven-year-old parenting anxious teenagers while supporting a parent through wildfire-driven displacement, with grief running quietly underneath everything. Picture a seventy-eight-year-old who can describe in detail the trout creek behind her childhood farm — and the dry bed it has become. The same climate change registers very differently across those four lives. Children and adolescents tend to report the highest levels of climate distress — they're aware but have limited agency. In early adulthood, climate emotions tangle with identity-defining decisions like career and parenting. In midlife, grief blends with frustration, and caregiving stretches you thin. And in older adulthood, you often see solastalgia for landscapes that have shifted within a single lifetime. Real lives don't fit neatly into stages, of course — but the lens helps us see what we might otherwise miss.

---

## SLIDE 33 — Population Impacts

Climate change does not affect everyone equally — and this is one of the most important things to hold onto in this work. Marginalized communities — and that includes Indigenous peoples, racialized communities, people living in poverty, persons with disabilities, gender-diverse people, and communities in the Global South — bear the greatest burden of climate impacts, while having contributed the least to the crisis. These inequities compound existing health disparities, and they create additional layers of climate-related emotional distress. The emotions in these communities aren't just bigger. They sit on top of histories of harm, displacement, and exclusion that long predate the climate crisis itself.

---

## SLIDE 34 — Intersectionality

This brings us to intersectionality — a concept developed by the legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw. Intersectionality describes how overlapping systems of power — racism, sexism, classism, ableism, colonialism, heteronormativity — combine to produce experiences that can't be explained by any single factor on its own. The shorthand is this: identities don't stack — they combine. Let me give you two illustrations. An Indigenous woman in a coastal community may carry colonial dispossession of her people's land, gendered caregiving expectations during disasters, and economic constraints on her ability to relocate — all at once. Her climate grief isn't three separate griefs added together. It's a single, compounded experience. Or take a disabled Black man in a heat-island neighbourhood. He may face mobility barriers to cooling centres, racial disinvestment in his neighbourhood's infrastructure, and a medical system that has historically not believed his pain. That's ableism, racism, and economic inequality moving together. Intersectionality does two important things for our work here. It sharpens our diagnosis — it helps us see why generic interventions fail communities at the margins. And it points us toward better solutions — culturally grounded, community-led, designed *with* rather than *for* the people most affected.

And fourth, marginalization and intersectionality create compounded climate distress, which means our responses need to be equity-centred or they will simply fail the people who most need them.

## SLIDE 38 — Adaptive vs. Maladaptive

Not all responses to climate distress are equally helpful. The key distinction we work with is between adaptive and maladaptive responses. Adaptive responses preserve our wellbeing and foster meaningful action over time. Maladaptive responses lead to paralysis, avoidance, or destructive patterns. And here's the crucial part: the emotions themselves are not the problem. It's how we respond to them that matters. Adaptive responses look like constructive problem-solving, seeking out social support, channelling anxiety into advocacy, finding meaning in collective action, and regulating our emotions while staying engaged. Maladaptive responses look like chronic avoidance and denial, catastrophizing without action, suppressing emotions until we burn out, isolating ourselves, or sliding into nihilism. The balance we're looking for is between acknowledging the severity of what's happening and maintaining the capacity to act. That balance is really the heart of adaptive coping. Emotions aren't obstacles to action — when we channel them constructively, they're often the fuel for it.

---

## SLIDE 39 — Three Types of Coping

There are three types of coping I want you to know about, and all three are useful — you really do need all three. The first is problem-focused coping. This is taking direct action to address the source of stress — home preparedness, joining a community adaptation effort, lobbying for a policy change, shifting your personal consumption. It's most effective when the stressor is at least partially within your control, and what it does is convert helplessness into purposeful engagement. The second is emotion-focused coping. This is regulating your emotional responses when an external situation feels difficult to change — talk therapy, time in green spaces, mindfulness, expressive writing, comfort from trusted relationships. This kind of coping prevents burnout and emotional numbness. The third is meaning-focused coping. This is finding purpose in adversity — reframing activism as a moral calling, connecting climate action to your values and identity, deriving meaning from intergenerational responsibility. Meaning-focused coping is what sustains long-term engagement. The most effective approach blends all three flexibly. You might volunteer for a resilience initiative — that's problem-focused. You attend a climate grief circle — that's emotion-focused. You frame your work as part of a larger movement for justice — that's meaning-focused. They reinforce each other.

---

## SLIDE 40 — Four Levels of Action

Coping with climate emotions is rarely a solo project. Sustainable resilience grows when action is happening at four interlocking levels at the same time. The first is the individual level — grounding, breathing, time in nature, news limits, journaling, naming your own emotions. For example, setting a twenty-minute news cap and going for a daily walk. The second is the organizational and familial level — climate-informed conversations, workplace policies, trauma-informed teams, mutual care agreements, modelling honest dialogue with children. For example, a weekly check-in question with your kids: anything climate is bringing up? The third is the community level — climate cafés, mutual aid, local restoration, faith gatherings, peer skill shares. For example, joining a neighbourhood emergency-prep group, or attending a local climate grief circle. And the fourth is the systemic and structural level — mental health funding for climate-affected regions, equitable adaptation policy, health-system protocols, curriculum reform, advocacy. For example, writing your MP about a just-transition policy, or supporting a candidate. Strategies at any one level have limits. The four levels together are what carry people through over the long term.

## SLIDE 41 — The Role of Hope

I want to spend a moment specifically on hope, because in the climate context it's not a luxury — it's a necessity. Researchers have described several distinct forms of hope. There's Radical Hope, from the philosopher Jonathan Lear — the belief in a future good that we cannot yet fully imagine. There's Active Hope, from Joanna Macy — a participatory stance, not waiting for hope to arrive but practising it, through awareness, gratitude, and creative collaboration. There's Stubborn Optimism, from Christiana Figueres — clear-eyed acceptance of the facts alongside a determined conviction that solutions exist. And there's Hope Theory, from C.R. Snyder — a more structured approach involving goal-setting, pathway thinking, and agency thinking. These are different angles on the same insight. Hope is something you do, not something that happens to you.

## SLIDE 42 — Resilience Strategies

Now let's get practical. Validation comes first — before any of us can cope effectively, we have to actually acknowledge what we're feeling. Validation reduces isolation, it builds trust, and it creates the psychological safety we need for honest engagement. From there, a few specific approaches. Nature-based practices — forest bathing, gardening, outdoor recreation, even just time near green spaces — support emotional regulation and restore our sense of connectedness. Somatic and movement-based practices — yoga, tai chi, progressive muscle relaxation, dance — release tension and regulate the nervous system. Social connection — support groups, climate cafés, peer networks, collective action — counters isolation and builds collective efficacy. And then there are grounding techniques like box breathing, the five-four-three-two-one senses exercise, cold water on the hands. These are small, learnable, repeatable. And one important reminder: collective coping is, in the long run, often more sustaining than solo coping. Climate emotions are shared emotions — they tend to respond best to shared practice.

## SLIDE 43 — Therapeutic Approaches

When climate distress crosses into clinical territory — chronic anxiety, traumatic stress, persistent functional impairment — there are several established therapeutic modalities that have been adapted for climate-related concerns. None of them is a cure-all. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy examines and reframes the thought patterns that intensify distress. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy is particularly well-matched here, because it doesn't try to argue away difficult emotions — it helps people accept what they feel and commit to action that aligns with their values. Narrative Therapy separates the person from the problem. Ecotherapy and other nature-based approaches conduct therapeutic work in or with the natural world. Trauma-focused approaches like EMDR are appropriate for clients with post-traumatic stress from direct climate disasters. Mindfulness-based therapies strengthen our capacity to be with difficult emotions. And group-based and community approaches — climate cafés, Joanna Macy's Work That Reconnects — address the loneliness component. For most non-clinical climate distress, peer support and community engagement are the right starting place. Therapeutic referral becomes appropriate when distress is persistent, or when trauma hasn't resolved with time and support.

## SLIDE 43b — Ten Principles for Climate Mental Health

Before we close the module, I want to anchor everything we've just covered in a recent synthesis from the literature. In their 2024 evidence review for the Mental Health and Climate Change Alliance, Higgs, Palmer-Fluevog, and Card identified ten principles for climate-mental-health work. They run from accurate climate literacy, through emotional diversity and inspired optimism, into social connection, nature bonding, and expanded coping repertoires, and out to climate justice, trauma-informed and culturally safe practice, community-led adaptation, and a collective-responsibility orientation. You'll recognize these as the threads we have been weaving through the module — and through the course as a whole. Hold them as a checklist for your own practice, and as a guide whenever you're supporting someone else.

## SLIDE 44 — Module 4 Takeaways

Five things to carry forward from this module. First, adaptive responses preserve wellbeing and foster action; the emotions themselves are not the problem — our responses to them are. Second, effective coping blends problem-focused, emotion-focused, and meaning-focused strategies, flexibly. Third, sustainable resilience requires action at all four levels — individual, organizational or familial, community, and systemic. Fourth, hope — radical, active, stubborn — is essential for sustained engagement. And fifth, validation is foundational. From there, practical skills like grounding, nature, somatic practices, and community engagement are what build resilience over time.

## SLIDE 47 — Ethical Frameworks

Climate change is no longer a distant or abstract issue. It's affecting the people and communities that professionals across all sectors serve. So integrating mental health awareness into professional practice isn't optional anymore — it's an ethical imperative. There are four frameworks that can guide that responsibility. The first is the Ethics of Care, from Carol Gilligan, which emphasizes our relational interdependence and the moral obligation we have to those affected. The second is the Common Good framework, which prioritizes communal wellbeing over individual or corporate interest. The third is the Whole-of-Society approach, articulated by the United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction, which recognizes that no single sector alone can address climate challenges. And the fourth is Systems Thinking, from Peter Senge, which highlights how climate impacts ripple across sectors and require interdisciplinary collaboration. Different doors into the same room — all of them leading us toward shared responsibility.

## SLIDE 48 — Educators

Let's start with educators. Educators play an essential role in fostering climate literacy while addressing the emotional dimensions of climate change. The key strategies here include integrating Social-Emotional Learning with climate education, using age-appropriate approaches, facilitating emotional expression through art, journaling, and group discussion, and using experiential and place-based learning — things like tree planting, water conservation projects, local ecosystem exploration — which foster a direct emotional connection. Encouraging student engagement in climate movements builds agency and counters helplessness. A few concrete examples to make this real. A grade-school teacher running a climate-action club where students channel eco-anxiety into building a pollinator garden. A high-school counsellor pairing climate-literacy curriculum with structured reflection circles after extreme-weather events. Or a school district that adopts a trauma-informed response plan for wildfire- or flood-affected communities.
